Motivation, background and context

At my first working experience as a mechanical engineer in a manufacturing company for the railway industry I has been requested to perform static structural analysis on riveted structures. It was a riveted structure and I’m talking about a couple hundreds rivets.

Not a big deal you would say. Well it was a big deal, a very big one since the engineer who was in charge of the analysis specifically told me to prepare the model in Ansys Mechanical by defining the riveted joints as revolute joints for each individual rivet. I spent an entire afternoon manually selecting and coupling the edges of the parts in correspondence of the rivet joint to correctly defining the coupling between the parts. And the worst part of the tale is that that senior engineer told me he had spent months performing similar tasks.

At this point one could basically choose between two different philosophies of life: simply get the job done or go through the effort of finding a better, and possibly cleverer, way of getting the job done. I’m more of the second philosophy.

Challenges I faced

So, to put things clear, I’ve got a 3D model with a few dozen sheet metal parts with corresponding holes for the rivets that should keep everything together and I’m in charge of individually define a revolute joint in Ansys Mechanical for each rivet to prepare the model for the structural analysis. I spent an afternoon manually performing the task and studying the command palette in desperate hope to find some useful automation and finally I got home with more than half the job wet to be done.

As you probably understood by this time I’m not the type to give up easily. As soon as I got home I started tackling the problem my own way.
